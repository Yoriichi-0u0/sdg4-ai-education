Case 1

Connecting the access gap to philosophy

Ethics

Ethics gives the first lens. Distributive justice asks whether the basic means of learning are fairly allocated. If practice time and feedback depend on family income or postcode, the system is not only unequal but unjust. A rights based view treats baseline access to devices, connectivity, and assistive tools as part of the right to learn, which aligns with child centered guidance from international bodies and with national recovery strategies that put foundational learning first (UNICEF, 2020; World Bank, 2025).

Logic

Logic helps to clean up the conversation. Many debates fall into a false choice that frames learning as in person or digital. The better design is blended and offline first. Another error treats possession of a device as equivalent to effective access. A phone with no data and a failing battery does not provide the same opportunity as a stable laptop connection. Clearer reasoning avoids shallow indicators and keeps the focus on time on task and quality of feedback (Avanesian et al., 2021; UNICEF & ITU, 2020).

Epistemology

Epistemology asks what counts as evidence of learning when participation is constrained. Missing submissions do not always mean missing understanding. Camera off does not always mean disengagement. Assessment needs multiple low bandwidth ways for students to show what they know, for example short voice notes, photos of handwritten work, or quick offline quizzes that sync later. This keeps grades reliable and keeps knowledge tied to performance rather than to bandwidth (UNICEF Office of Research Innocenti, 2025; UNICEF, 2020).

Critical perspectives

Critical perspectives warn against techno solutionism. One device per child does not fix pedagogy, maintenance, language access, or teacher workload. Digital colonialism can appear when platforms ignore local languages and contexts. A reflective stance favors locally relevant content, shared governance of data, and partnerships that build capacity rather than dependency, which is a theme across child rights and equity focused guidance on digital learning (UNICEF Office of Research Innocenti, 2025; UNICEF UK, 2021).

Case 2

Philosophical checks for AI tutors

Ethics

Beneficence asks whether AI tutors improve learning for all, not only students with the latest devices. Non-maleficence requires that foreseeable harms such as biased or false outputs are avoided. Justice demands equal opportunity to benefit, which means pairing tools with access support and privacy protections for minors (UNESCO, 2023; UNICEF, 2021).

Logic

AI can produce fluent but invalid arguments. Students should be taught to check premises, detect non sequiturs, and verify citations. Teachers can use AI outputs as fallacy-spotting exercises, helping learners identify weak inference and repair the argument. Policy briefs stress the importance of critical evaluation of AI responses rather than uncritical acceptance (U.S. Department of Education, 2023).

Epistemology

When an AI can draft an answer instantly, reliable knowledge still requires justified, true belief supported by evidence and process. Classroom routines should require students to show steps, explain why a method works, and cross-check sources so that AI becomes a tool for inquiry instead of a substitute for understanding (UNESCO, 2023).

Ideology and decolonisation

Avoid techno-solutionism and imported defaults that ignore local curricula and languages. Prioritise culturally relevant content, shared data governance, and capacity building so schools are not locked into opaque systems. International guidance argues for human-centred, context-aware deployment that respects national priorities and community values (UNESCO, 2023; OECD, 2023).
