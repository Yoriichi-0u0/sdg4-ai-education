Case 1

Access is shaped by infrastructure, skills, and policy

Access is shaped first by infrastructure and affordability. Patchy broadband or 4G, unreliable electricity, and high data costs make simple tasks like watching a short explanation or joining a ten minute check in feel risky. Families that own a single smartphone cannot serve multiple school schedules, and topping up data competes with essentials (UNICEF & ITU, 2020; GSMA Intelligence, 2024).

School readiness and digital skills add a second layer. Device pools are small, technical support is thin, and materials are not always accessible by default. Parents and students who are new to learning platforms spend time on navigation and account issues, which reduces time on tasks that actually build knowledge (UNICEF, 2020; UNICEF Office of Research Innocenti, 2025).

Policy design is the third piece. Few systems guarantee a measurable minimum level of access. Procurement for assistive technology is slow or one off, and emergency device programs often lack maintenance budgets. Environmental factors such as power cuts and monsoon season outages make continuity harder unless the whole system is planned for low bandwidth and offline use. Countries that treat foundational access as part of foundational learning see more stable recovery and better equity outcomes (World Bank, 2025; UNICEF UK, 2021).

Case 2

Adoption, governance, and equity guardrails

Adoption is driven by real classroom pressures. Teachers juggle large classes, diverse reading levels, and limited time for individual feedback. AI tutors promise extra practice sets, hints, and explanations on demand. When these tools align with curriculum goals and are checked for quality, they can increase time on task and help students correct errors sooner, a mechanism that underpins learning gains (Ma et al., 2014; American Psychological Association).

Technology maturity and governance shape outcomes. General-purpose models are powerful but can hallucinate facts and produce plausible-sounding mistakes. Policy guidance emphasises a human-in-the-loop approach, transparency about when AI is used, and safeguards against bias and harmful content. Systems should be auditable, age-appropriate, and designed so teachers can see what the tool suggested and why (UNESCO, 2023; OECD, 2023).

Equity and privacy are the other major factors. If AI tutors require high bandwidth, new devices, or broad data collection, they deepen access gaps and create unnecessary risks for minors. Child-centred guidance calls for data minimisation, clear consent, and the best interests of the child as the primary design principle. National regulators add age-appropriate design standards that edtech tools should meet before use in schools (UNICEF, 2021; UK Information Commissioner’s Office, 2020).
